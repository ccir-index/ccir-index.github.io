Why GPU Credit Agreements Need a Named Reference Rate

GPU-backed lending has scaled past $20 billion in committed facilities without a named independent reference rate in credit documentation. The financial infrastructure — loan covenants, borrowing base formulas, LTV triggers, and collateral disposition standards — requires a market-standardized rate built for citation in agreements. CRI‑H100 is that rate.

Market Rate vs. Book Value
Book depreciation does not move with the rental market

GPU rental rates have declined materially since 2024. Revenue covenants and borrowing base formulas that reference the live on-demand market need an independent, named rate to anchor those calculations — one both parties can verify independently.

Independent Reference Rate
A rate defined by either party creates documentation risk

A rate defined by the lender favors the lender. A rate defined by the borrower favors the borrower. Syndicated facilities need a rate all lenders can independently verify. Post-LIBOR, compliance functions have developed institutional resistance to reference rates that lack independent governance and published methodology. CRI-H100 is structured to meet that standard.

Contract Renewal Risk
Contract renewal risk is the key credit signal — and requires an independent rate to measure

In a contracted-revenue structure, the most important surveillance question is the spread between the borrower’s base contracted rate and the current on-demand market. When CRI-H100 falls toward the borrower’s base contracted rate, renewal leverage erodes — a Convergence Notice signal. When CRI-H100 falls well below contracted rates with near-term expiry concentration, the re-pricing cliff widens — a Reset Cliff signal. Both require an independent reference rate that neither party controls.

Workout & Disposition
UCC §9-627 requires an independent rate to defend collateral disposition

Under UCC §9-627, a disposition is commercially reasonable only if conducted at “the price current in any recognized market.” Without a named independent rate, a lender cannot defend GPU collateral disposition against a borrower who argues the sale was commercially unreasonable — and under UCC §9-626, a commercially unreasonable disposition may eliminate the deficiency judgment. CRI-H100, cited in the credit agreement, provides the independent market reference that survives that scrutiny.

Profile A — Contracted Revenue Operator
8,000 × H100 SXM5  ·  $200M outstanding  ·  Gross contracted $3.80/hr  ·  Base contracted $2.90/hr (after Premium Schedule deductions)
§7.02
Collateral Coverage / LTV
Marks the pledged GPU fleet to CRI-H100 monthly using a cap rate formula — not book depreciation, not contracted revenue. CV = GPUs × CRI-H100 × 8,760 hrs × 72% utilization ÷ 28% cap rate. Cap rate steps to 35% on Generation Convergence Event. LTV maintained below 80%.
Financial covenant
§7.03
Generation Spread
Monitors (CRI-B200 − CRI-H100) ÷ CRI-H100 as a proxy for H100 training-tier obsolescence. When the spread falls below 25% for two consecutive dates, the §7.02 Capitalization Rate steps from 28% to 35% — tightening the collateral mark without an amendment.
Surveillance → feeds §7.02
§7.01
Contract Renewal Risk
Two-sided surveillance over a rolling 6-period forward window. Convergence Notice at CPR below 1.10× — Base Contracted Rate approaching on-demand market rate, renewal leverage eroding. Reset Cliff Notice when CPR exceeds 1.50× and Forward Renewal Exposure exceeds 30% of contracted GPU-hours — wide spread with near-term expiry concentration signals a material re-pricing cliff. Both triggers require the same disclosure: expiring contracts by customer, renewal prospects, and projected revenue under a re-pricing scenario at current CRI-H100.
Contract renewal risk surveillance · disclosure only
Profile B — Mixed-Book Operator
1,500 × H100 SXM5  ·  $30M outstanding  ·  70% training / 30% inference at baseline
§7.01
Market Capture Ratio
Measures whether the borrower’s actual revenue per GPU achieves a minimum percentage of CRI-H100. Detects underpricing, underutilization, or customer mix deterioration. A coverage ratio applied to an on-demand market operator is nearly inert; the Market Capture Ratio instead asks: is the borrower getting market rates? Rate compression is detected by §7.02. Both signals are needed; neither substitutes for the other.
Operational efficiency · 85% floor
§7.02
Collateral Coverage / LTV
Marks the pledged GPU fleet to CRI-H100 monthly using a cap rate formula. CV = GPUs × CRI-H100 × 8,760 hrs × 72% utilization ÷ 28% cap rate. Cap rate steps to 35% on Generation Convergence Event. CRI-H100 is the single observable input that updates each period; all other parameters are fixed at closing and independently verifiable by any party.

§7.03
Workload Composition
Requires borrower certification of training vs. inference workload mix each Calculation Date. If Training Workload Percentage falls below 50% for two consecutive periods, a Blended Utilization Rate adjusts the §7.02 Collateral Value (72% training / 65% inference, weighted). Surfaces workload migration before it propagates into a §7.02 deficiency — the borrower can migrate workloads while reporting identical headline utilization.
Surveillance → adjusts §7.02
§7.04
Generation Spread
Monitors (CRI-B200 − CRI-H100) ÷ CRI-H100 as a proxy for H100 training-tier obsolescence. When the spread falls below 25% for two consecutive dates, the §7.02 Capitalization Rate steps from 28% to 35% — tightening the collateral mark without an amendment. CCIR publishes both rates daily; the threshold and step-up mechanics are credit judgments set at underwriting.

The defining Profile A asymmetry: rate compression tightens §7.02 (hardware marks down) while §7.01 widens the spread (locked contracts hold above a falling on-demand rate floor). Both must be monitored. The inflection is contract renewal — when the borrower re-contracts into a depressed market, the CPR compresses rapidly toward the Convergence Notice threshold. Lenders should model the combined stress scenario at origination: §7.03 cap rate step-up compounds in §7.02, and §7.01 Reset Cliff disclosure surfaces the re-pricing exposure before it propagates into a deficiency. Each of those signals individually fires a notice; the combined stress is what happens when all three were ignored.

The key Profile B distinction: for a mixed-book operator, a falling CRI-H100 rate tightens both §7.01 Market Capture and §7.02 Collateral Coverage simultaneously — there is no locked-contract cushion. The Market Underperformance scenario is the clearest demonstration of §7.01’s purpose: CRI is flat, hardware value is flat, but the borrower is underpricing. §7.01 fires; §7.02 does not. Without §7.01, this borrower delivers a clean borrowing base certificate while quietly losing market position. In the Combined Stress scenario, §7.01 still passes (the borrower is efficiently capturing a depressed market) — the problem is hardware value and workload mix, not operational execution.
The covenants above serve their primary function during the facility term. The named independent rate also matters at default: under UCC §9-627, a collateral disposition is commercially reasonable only if conducted at the price current in a recognized market. A named rate, cited in the credit agreement and verifiable by any party, provides that reference without post-hoc reconstruction — and because CCIR’s methodology is open and reproducible, an expert witness can independently replicate the rate calculation. Under UCC §9-626, a commercially unreasonable disposition may eliminate the deficiency judgment right. The citation that supports covenant surveillance during the facility term is the same citation that makes the disposition defensible if it does not.
