Market Price Monitoring Covenant
Market Price Monitoring — Any GPU Facility
Add to any existing GPU facility. No covenant renegotiation required.
A Market Price Monitoring covenant is a market information covenant — no economic triggers, no structural changes. It anchors the facility to a live, independent reference rate from day one, giving the lender early signal when collateral economics are shifting and giving the borrower a structured framework for demonstrating that the asset still supports the credit.
Two thresholds are set at closing relative to CRI-8G for the pledged GPU model. When the rate crosses a threshold on two consecutive Calculation Dates, the borrower delivers notice. The lender gets a documented, timestamped data point tied to an independent benchmark — the kind of record that survives a portfolio review, a syndication, or a workout.
Threshold Trigger Consequence
Level 1 CRI-8G falls below 75% of CRI-8G at closing for two consecutive Calculation Dates Market Price Notice — borrower delivers written notice within 5 business days
Level 2 CRI-8G falls below 50% of CRI-8G at closing for two consecutive Calculation Dates Market Price Alert — borrower delivers enhanced notice including fleet utilization and re-pricing impact assessment
Illustrative Example
Closing CRI-8G (H100): $2.95/GPU/hr
Level 1 trigger: CRI-8G < $2.21 for two consecutive dates
Level 2 trigger: CRI-8G < $1.48 for two consecutive dates

If CRI-8G publishes at $2.15 on two consecutive Calculation Dates, a Level 1 Market Price Notice is triggered. The borrower delivers notice. The lender now has a documented, independently sourced data point for portfolio monitoring — and the borrower has a documented record of timely disclosure tied to a named benchmark.

Borrowing Base Certificate
Borrowing Base — Per-Model CRI-8G Collateral Valuation
How much the borrower can draw, recalculated every Determination Date.
The borrowing base converts per-model CRI-8G rates into a collateral value using a capitalization rate approach, then applies an advance rate to determine the borrower's available credit. Each Eligible GPU Model contributes independently — a mixed H100/B200 fleet rolls up into a single aggregate borrowing base anchored to live, per-model on-demand market rates.
Component Formula Illustrative Value
Per-Model Collateral Value (GPU Count × CRI-8G Rate × Utilization × 8,760 hrs) ÷ Cap Rate H100: $63.1M  |  B200: $47.6M
Aggregate Collateral Value Σ Per-Model Collateral Values $110.6M
Borrowing Base Aggregate Collateral Value × Advance Rate (65%) − Reserves $71.9M
Rate Cap Scenario — §7.02(d)
At $45M outstanding against a $71.9M borrowing base (40.7% LTV), the facility has $26.9M of cushion in the base case. A uniform −35% stress across both models narrows that to +$1.8M. A −50% stress produces a $9.1M deficiency at 81.4% LTV — breaching the 80% covenant. The certificate includes full stress tables at −25%, −35%, and −50%.

GPU Credit Framework
Three-Covenant Framework — CRI-8G Benchmark Series
Illustrative applications of CRI-8G rates in credit structures.
The GPU Credit Framework maps three distinct credit risks to three covenants, each anchored to per-model CRI-8G rates. Together they give lenders continuous visibility across revenue quality, collateral value, and contract renewal risk — the three dimensions that drive credit performance in GPU-backed facilities.
Covenant What It Monitors CRI-8G Role
§7.01 Revenue Coverage — ratio of actual GPU revenue to CRI-implied market revenue. Tests whether contracted rates still exceed what the on-demand market would pay. Denominator: CRI-8G × pledged GPUs × hours = market revenue baseline
§7.02 Collateral Coverage / LTV — marks the pledged GPU fleet to current on-demand market levels using a capitalization rate approach, not book depreciation. Numerator input: CRI-8G rate × utilization × hours ÷ cap rate = per-model collateral value
§7.03 Contract Renewal Risk — monitors the spread between the borrower's contracted rate and CRI-8G per model across a rolling forward window. Denominator: CRI-8G rate per model. Convergence and reset-cliff triggers fire per model, not fleet-weighted.
Key Asymmetry
A declining CRI-8G rate tightens §7.02 Collateral Coverage (the hardware backstop marks down) while §7.03 Contract Renewal Risk may improve (existing contracts look richer relative to a falling market). The three covenants are designed to capture this asymmetry — no single metric covers all three risk dimensions.

Workout & Liquidation Reference
Disposition Pricing — CRI-8G as Recovery Anchor
The collateral is already deployed. CRI-8G is the listing price.
In the SPV structures that underpin rated and syndicated GPU facilities, the SPV holds title to the hardware and controls the hosting relationship. On default, the lender or its agent redirects deployed capacity to the open rental market without reconfiguring the hardware, shipping, or negotiating new colocation terms. The GPUs stay where they are. The revenue stream redirects. CRI-8G is the rate at which that capacity is listed.
This is the recovery story the market already practices — and CRI-8G makes it documentable. The lender has a named, independent reference rate on the disposition date anchoring the commercially reasonable price defense, not an internal model or stale appraisal reconstructed after the fact.
Recovery Phase What Happens CRI-8G Role
Triage Generation Spread signals whether to remarket current-gen capacity or write down and pursue next-gen recovery Wide B200/H100 spread = H100 still commands training-tier premium. Compressed or inverted = market has moved on.
Listing SPV lists deployed capacity on the open on-demand market at current rates — no physical disposition required CRI-8G sets the listing price for immediate on-demand rental
Documentation Realized recovery documented relative to an independently published market rate on the disposition date Named benchmark citation supporting the commercially reasonable price defense
Illustrative Realization Framework
CRI-8G provides the headline rate. Lenders apply their own realization factor.

In an SPV structure with in-place hosting, the realization factor reflects time to redirect revenue, market depth for the chip model, and any re-contracting friction — not physical logistics. SPV redeployment will typically support a higher realization factor than structures requiring hardware removal.

The framework is: CRI-8G × Realization Factor × Generation Adjustment = Recovery Estimate. CCIR provides the first input. The realization factor and generation adjustment are credit judgments set by the lender.
Bilateral / Pledge Structures
In bilateral facilities where the lender perfects a UCC-1 lien on the hardware but does not control the hosting relationship, recovery requires negotiating continued hosting with the facility operator or physically repossessing for resale. CRI-8G serves as the market-rate reference against which bids are evaluated and commercially reasonable sale price is demonstrated.

Where Current Documentation Falls Short
Four reasons GPU collateral deals need a purpose-built rate.
01
Collateral marked to historical assumptions, not current market levels

H100 on-demand access rates have declined materially since 2024. Without a live reference rate, covenant signals arrive only after value has already moved.

02
No trigger for the revenue transition already underway

The signal is not utilization — it is revenue quality. As contracted training revenue decays toward inference-tier rates, risk increases before coverage ratios show it.

03
GPU collateral evolves faster than the documentation was built for

Generation transitions are scheduled market events, not tail risks. Most current structures lack real-time mechanisms to respond as they occur.

04
No defensible market price at disposition

At disposition, lenders must demonstrate that collateral was sold at a commercially reasonable market price. Without a named, independent reference rate cited in the agreement, that price must be reconstructed after the fact — introducing litigation risk and weakening recovery claims.

All four gaps stem from the absence of a consistent, observable market reference rate usable across underwriting, monitoring, and disposition.
